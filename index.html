Abstract

The main challenge in learning image-conditioned robotic policies is acquiring a visual representation conducive to low-level control. Due to the high dimensionality of the image space, learning a good visual representation requires a considerable amount of visual data. However, when learning in the real world, data is expensive. Sim2Real is a promising paradigm for overcoming data scarcity in the real-world target domain by using a simulator to collect large amounts of cheap data closely related to the target task. However, it is difficult to transfer an image-conditioned policy from sim to real when the domains are very visually dissimilar. To bridge the sim2real visual gap, we propose using natural language descriptions of images as a unifying signal across domains that captures the underlying task-relevant semantics. Our key insight is that if two image observations from different domains are labeled with similar language, the policy should predict similar action distributions for both images. We demonstrate that training the image encoder to predict the language description or the distance between descriptions of a sim or real image serves as a useful, data- efficient pretraining step that helps learn a domain-invariant image representation. We can then use this image encoder as the backbone of an IL policy trained simultaneously on a large amount of simulated and a handful of real demonstrations. Our approach outperforms widely used prior sim2real methods and strong vision-language pretraining baselines like CLIP and R3M by 25 to 40%.

Overview

Lang4sim2real adopts the common pretrain-then-finetune learning paradigm. We first pretrain an image backbone encoder on language-annotated images from both simulation and real-world demonstrations of a task. This will facilitate policy learning later by preserving the semantic similarity of scenes in images between the two domains. Then, we freeze this encoder and train a policy network composed of trainable adapter modules and a policy head to perform behavioral cloning (BC) on action-labeled data from both domains.

Baselines

We evaluate against three classes of baselines: no pretraining, vision-language pretrained baselins, and other sim2real methods. We find our method outperforms all 3. Given 100 real demonstrations, it increases performance by an average of 30 percent over the best no pretraining baseline in which we initialize the CNN from stratch and train via BC on either just real, or both simulated and real demonstrations. Compared to R3M as a pretrained baseline, it increases perfomacne over 30% and over 50% compared to CLIP. It also outperforms all 3 of our prior sim2real baselines by an even higher margin, including MMD, domain randomization, and Automatic Domain Randomization with Random Network Adversary.


Ablations

We perform an ablation to determine the effects of language by using an alternate pretraining objective of classifying the stage of the trajectory rather than regressing to a language annotation. We find that language provides a measurable benefit, especially in multi-step pick-and-place, perhaps because pretraining with language leverages similarities language descriptions between the first and second steps of the pick-and-place task.
